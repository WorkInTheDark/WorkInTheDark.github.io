# Research Is Not Homework

##### *Chapter 0 Episode 1*

Your entire academic life before stepping into research may look roughly like this: *for every course you take, the instructor introduces a new topic every week, assigns a problem set as homework, you complete it by the deadline, and the cycle resets the following week*. Whether or not the topic interests you, the syllabus was decided before the semester began, and the course structure carries you forward. More importantly, the answers to your homework questions are unchanging, and the instructor knows the correct answers to most, if not every, question a student might raise about the course material. Your only duty is to demonstrate that you have learned the material the way you are instructed.

If you are considering a research career, or have just started one, or simply want to try it out, the most important thing I want to share is that **research breaks every piece of the aforementioned life cycle**. Recognizing how and why research differs from the conventional academic life is the first step toward the joy of research, because researchers who carry the "coursework" mindset into research can struggle and get lost in predictable ways.

### Nobody Has the "Gold" Answer

In a typical course, the instructors know the answers to the problem sets before assigning them to you; otherwise, I don't see a feasible way to quantitatively assess your work. Nevertheless, in research, the whole novelty and validity of your work will be voided if you are just *reinventing the wheel*. The point of research is that nobody has solved the particular question you are focusing on, which means the entire community, including your advisor and me, does not have a concrete, gold answer.

What does it mean? It means you cannot hand your solution to your advisor and expect them to check it against a gold answer, and your advisor does not have a gold answer if you ask, "Is this the right approach?"

As you continue to build up your knowledge and expertise in a specific research domain, you will gradually become one of a small number of people who deeply understand what has been accomplished and what remains open. Even for a single research project, the effort you have spent searching, identifying, and reading relevant literature will pay off in a way that shifts the dynamic between you and your advisor. Your advisor likely knows less about the fine-grained details of your research topic than you do. They may have read the same papers, but they did not invest comparable time and effort into your specific research question beyond the weekly project meeting.

What an experienced advisor can contribute to your project and to your personal development is a different kind of value. They bring familiarity with related work from adjacent or even distant fields that you may not have encountered, experiences (often learned through failures) about which research directions tend to be productive, and the capability to critique the rationales behind your plan from a reviewer's perspective.

For prospective researchers with no prior research experience, this shift in mental model about research can feel disorienting and sometimes frustrating. You may find yourself asking for clear to-do tasks, only to realize they never come, or feeling anxious that you are "doing it wrong" because nobody has confirmed your direction. I want to reaffirm that the anxiety is very normal, and it fades rapidly as you develop confidence through active research engagement. I have seen many researchers who felt anxious at first but later found genuine enjoyment in independent exploration, precisely because they discovered the joy of investigating things that no one has explored before, and learned to treat the "failures" along the path as guidance rather than setbacks.

### The Coursework Trap

What follows in this section matters most to those who want to thrive in the process of doing research, not only in the awards that come from it.

The most common failure mode I see among new researchers is what I call the "**coursework trap**." Researchers treat each project milestone as an assignment to complete under time constraints, optimizing for the minimum effort or the easiest path to meet the bar, and then wait for the next assignment. A researcher in the coursework trap does exactly what was asked or agreed upon in a project meeting, nothing more, and arrives at the next meeting expecting new orders. When no clear assignment is given, the research progress stalls.

Researchers in the coursework trap can still get their work done and their papers published, for sure. The slowdown in research progress is the most obvious consequence, but it is among the least important ones. I want to discuss two other aspects and the rationales behind them.

The first is about the **mutual respect** expected among team members. For most experienced researchers, if not all, the motivation for participating in a research project is not that they need another paper in their portfolio. For me, for example, it is often because I appreciate the opportunity to work and talk with people I truly respect, and I especially appreciate that they are willing to dedicate a portion of their time each week to work with me, given that there are so many great research opportunities out there, so many talented people to work with, and so many fun things to do that have nothing to do with research at all. The intellectual engagement during those 30 minutes of a project meeting, and the excitement and joy behind it, far exceeds what I can get from, for instance, playing a video game. If you choose to be reactive on a project, you are giving up the chance to continue working with these people in the future; if you engage actively, your excitement becomes contagious, and it would be hard for anyone to explain why they would not want to keep working with this type of researcher.

The second aspect is about **personal development**. If you have talked with successful researchers (I do not strictly constrain how you define "success" here), you would easily discover that their thoughts are extremely sharp, their insights are visionary, and the information density during the discussion is considerable. To me, these qualities are genuinely admirable, and I have wanted to become this kind of researcher from the first day I started doing research. The researchers who thrive are the ones who come to meetings not with questions alone, but with a clear report of **what they have attempted since the last meeting, the rationales behind their choices, what worked and what did not, what they think the results mean, and what they plan to do next**. You can easily spot the difference in information density between these two types of researchers from my description. Over time, the difference compounds (I may write a separate post on why "compound interest" applies to research as well) as each cycle of proposing, executing, and reflecting forces you to think critically and evaluate rationally, making you a sharper researcher. Not to mention that the quality of your advisor's feedback improves in proportion to the quality of what you bring to the table.
